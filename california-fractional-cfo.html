When You Need a Fractional CFO

Common triggers for California businesses

Most companies engage a fractional CFO when they hit a stage where the financial function needs to grow before the business is ready to hire it full-time. The triggers are recognizable — and the cost of waiting is usually higher than the cost of engaging.

Preparing for a Capital Raise
Investors will scrutinize your financials. If your books aren't in GAAP order — or if your revenue recognition, equity structure, or reporting isn't clean — it becomes a diligence problem. A fractional CFO gets the financial house in order before that conversation starts.
Navigating a First Audit
A first GAAS audit requires preparation that most management teams haven't done before. A fractional CFO who understands audit methodology manages the process — PBC preparation, technical accounting positions, auditor coordination — so the engagement runs efficiently and closes on schedule.
Building Board-Ready Reporting
Board members and investors expect financial reporting that is consistent, accurate, and easy to interrogate. If your monthly package is manually assembled and hard to reconcile, a fractional CFO restructures it — automated, documented, and delivered on a defined schedule every month.
Month-End Close Taking Too Long
A close cycle that runs three weeks into the following month means decisions are being made on stale data. A fractional CFO diagnoses the bottlenecks, restructures the sequence, automates what can be automated, and gets close time under 10 business days.
Preparing for a Sale or Transaction
Buyers and their advisors will conduct detailed financial due diligence. Gaps in accounting, undocumented positions, or a messy close process become leverage against the seller. A fractional CFO cleans up the financial package before the process starts — protecting deal value.
Outgrown the Bookkeeper
When the business is complex enough that a bookkeeper or staff accountant can't keep up — or when lenders, investors, or auditors need more than they can produce — a fractional CFO bridges the gap without the cost and commitment of a full-time hire.
What's Included

What a fractional CFO engagement at LAC covers

Fractional CFO engagements at LAC are scoped to what you actually need — not a fixed package. Most engagements involve some combination of the following, depending on where the business is and where it needs to go.

Month-End Close Management
End-to-end oversight of the close cycle — task sequencing, preparer coordination, review of journal entries and reconciliations, and a structured close calendar that compresses timelines and distributes workload evenly across the month.
Financial Statement Preparation
GAAP-compliant financial statements with full footnote disclosures — monthly, quarterly, or annual — suitable for board distribution, lender reporting, investor review, or audit support. Technical standards (ASC 606, ASC 842, CECL) handled in-house.
Board & Investor Reporting
Monthly board packages — executive summary, financial results versus plan, key metrics, variance analysis — built from a single source of truth and delivered on a defined schedule. Templates designed for consistency across every reporting cycle.
Lender & Covenant Management
Debt covenant tracking, compliance certificate preparation, and lender relationship management. Reporting structured to meet lender requirements with clean, reconcilable support — and with enough lead time to address covenant issues before they become defaults.
Accounting Infrastructure & Automation
Rebuilding the systems underlying the financial function — consolidation workbooks, close automation, Power Query pipelines, Python scripts for multi-entity reporting. Deliverables that make the business's accounting faster and more reliable, not just this month's output.
Audit Coordination & Support
Managing the audit process end-to-end — PBC package preparation, technical accounting memo drafting, auditor correspondence, and status management through fieldwork and report issuance. Clients who go through this process typically run audits faster year over year.
